## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This document represents the final, corrected compilation of a multi-level research synthesis examining the role of macrophage polarization in regulating dormant disseminated tumor cells (DTCs). It incorporates findings from five postdoctoral briefings, two senior scientist integration reports, a Chief Scientist critical review, and an adversarial peer critique.

**Key Conclusions (Qualified):**

1. Macrophage polarization state is a significant modulator of DTC dormancy, with the strongest causal evidence in the lung niche (Dalla et al. 2024). Evidence in bone marrow, liver, and brain niches is incomplete, indirect, or absent. Macrophages should not be characterized as the "central rheostat" of dormancy across all organs without substantial further evidence.

2. The Extracellular Signal-Regulated Kinase (ERK)/p38 Mitogen-Activated Protein Kinase (p38 MAPK) signaling ratio is the best-characterized intracellular dormancy switch, but it is one component of a multi-pathway network that includes NR2F1, WNT, and HIPPO/YAP signaling. Single-cell data reveal heterogeneous ERK/p38 states within DTC populations.

3. Transforming Growth Factor Beta (TGF-beta) is among the most consistently implicated dormancy-associated cytokines, but its role is context-dependent: TGF-beta2 through TGF-beta Receptor Type III (TGF-betaRIII) on alveolar macrophages maintains dormancy (Dalla et al. 2024), while TGF-beta1 through Neuropilin 2 (NRP2) on tumor cells promotes dormancy exit (Recalde-Percaz et al. 2025).

4. The M1/M2 binary framework for macrophage polarization is acknowledged as an oversimplification. Macrophage activation exists on a multidimensional continuum. However, the binary remains in operational use because dormancy-relevant macrophage subsets have not been identified at the single-cell level.

5. No macrophage-targeting therapy has been tested with dormancy-specific endpoints in any model system or clinical trial. All therapeutic recommendations in this document are hypothesis-generating extrapolations, not evidence-based clinical guidance.

6. Citation integrity remediation was required: of 17 originally audited references, 3 were reclassified from unverifiable to confirmed upon secondary verification (Walker et al. 2019, Pereira et al. 2025, Darvishian et al. 2022), 2 were removed as unverifiable (He et al. 2026, Sun et al. 2023), 1 required journal correction (Zheng et al. 2023), and 4 require caveats or flags.

7. The premature clinical development roadmap (proposing a Phase 2 adjuvant trial of eganelisib) has been removed and replaced with a prioritized preclinical research agenda.

**Overall Assessment:** The research direction is sound. The mechanistic framework is coherent. The organ-specific analysis is the strongest and most novel component. However, the synthesis must be presented with appropriate humility about the gaps between preclinical observation and clinical translation. Every claim in this document is qualified by the weight of evidence supporting it.

## SECTION 2: INTEGRATED MECHANISTIC FRAMEWORK

### 2.1 The ERK/p38 Signaling Axis as a Central -- But Not Exclusive -- Intracellular Dormancy Switch

The ratio of Extracellular Signal-Activated Kinase (ERK) to p38 Mitogen-Activated Protein Kinase (p38 MAPK) activity within DTCs is the best-characterized intracellular arbiter of the dormancy-proliferation decision. High p38/low ERK signaling promotes cell cycle arrest and dormancy; a shift toward ERK dominance triggers proliferative outgrowth.

**ESTABLISHED:** The ERK/p38 ratio concept is well-supported across multiple experimental systems and originates from foundational work by Aguirre-Ghiso and colleagues spanning two decades. All contributing reports converge on this framework.

**CAVEATS:**
- The ratio is necessary but not sufficient to explain dormancy. Nuclear Receptor Subfamily 2 Group F Member 1 (NR2F1) acts upstream to reinforce this ratio. WNT signaling acts in parallel. HIPPO/Yes-Associated Protein (YAP) signaling is implicated in the brain niche.
- Single-cell data (Recalde-Percaz et al. 2025, confirmed) demonstrate heterogeneous ERK/p38 states within individual DTC populations. This heterogeneity means a subpopulation of DTCs may be poised for exit even when the average signaling profile suggests dormancy.
- The ERK/p38 ratio is measured by different methods across studies (phospho-specific Western blots, immunofluorescence, flow cytometry) with different antibodies, lysis conditions, and normalization strategies. There is no standardized quantitative threshold for "dormant" versus "proliferative."
- The ratio is better understood as one axis of a multi-dimensional signaling space rather than a binary switch.

**SPECULATIVE:** Whether the ERK/p38 ratio alone can be therapeutically targeted to maintain dormancy without affecting other essential cellular processes remains unknown.

### 2.2 NR2F1 as a Master Dormancy Regulator

Nuclear Receptor Subfamily 2 Group F Member 1 (NR2F1) is an orphan nuclear receptor that functions as a master transcriptional regulator of cellular dormancy. Borriello et al. (2022, confirmed) demonstrated that a specific subset of primary Tumor-Associated Macrophages (TAMs) at Tumor Microenvironment of Metastasis (TMEM) doorways simultaneously activates invasion (via MenaINV -- Mena Involved in Invasion, an isoform of the Mena/ENAH protein) and dormancy (NR2F1, SRY-Box Transcription Factor 9 [SOX9]) programs in disseminating tumor cells.

**ESTABLISHED:** NR2F1 induction by TMEM-associated macrophages is confirmed by Borriello et al. 2022 (PMID 35110548). NR2F1 reinforces the high-p38/low-ERK dormancy signaling state.

**CAVEATS:** The TMEM doorway mechanism was characterized in breast cancer. Its applicability to other cancer types is undetermined.

### 2.3 Macrophage-Derived Signals Modulating the Dormancy Switch

Macrophage-derived signals influence the ERK/p38 axis through multiple pathways:

#### 2.3.1 TGF-beta2 -- Dormancy Maintenance in the Lung

Transforming Growth Factor Beta 2 (TGF-beta2), produced by alveolar macrophages, sustains dormancy through TGF-beta Receptor Type III (TGF-betaRIII), activating downstream p38/Signal Transducer and Activator of Transcription 1 (STAT1) signaling (Dalla et al. 2024, confirmed). This is an organ-specific, tissue-resident macrophage function.

#### 2.3.2 TGF-beta1 -- Dormancy Exit via NRP2

Transforming Growth Factor Beta 1 (TGF-beta1), acting through Neuropilin 2 (NRP2) on tumor cells, promotes dormancy exit and proliferation (Recalde-Percaz et al. 2025, confirmed). This represents a context-dependent role for TGF-beta that contrasts with its dormancy-maintaining function via TGF-betaRIII.

**IMPORTANT NOTE:** TGF-beta is among the most consistently implicated dormancy-associated cytokines, but its role is not uniform. The isoform-specific effects (TGF-beta1 vs. TGF-beta2), receptor-specific signaling (TGF-betaRIII vs. NRP2), cellular source (alveolar macrophage vs. tumor cell), tissue context (lung vs. bone marrow vs. liver), and temporal dynamics (acute vs. chronic exposure) all modulate whether TGF-beta promotes dormancy maintenance or dormancy exit. Interferon gamma (IFN-gamma) has equally strong mechanistic evidence for dormancy induction (Pereira et al. 2025, confirmed). WNT5A, Bone Morphogenetic Protein 4/7 (BMP4/7), and C-X-C Motif Chemokine Ligand 10 (CXCL10) also have dormancy-enforcing evidence.

#### 2.3.3 IFN-gamma -- Dormancy Induction

Interferon gamma (IFN-gamma), produced by Cluster of Differentiation 8-positive T lymphocytes (CD8+ T cells) with macrophage amplification, directly induces dormancy via high p38/ERK ratio, activating STAT1 (Pereira et al. 2025, confirmed).

#### 2.3.4 IL-6/G-CSF -- Dormancy Exit

Interleukin 6 (IL-6) and Granulocyte Colony-Stimulating Factor (G-CSF), released from stromal cells following taxane-induced injury, activate MAPK/ERK Kinase (MEK)/ERK signaling to break dormancy (Ganesan et al. 2023, confirmed).

**SPECIFICITY NOTE:** This mechanism has been demonstrated specifically for taxane chemotherapy. It does not necessarily generalize to anthracyclines, platinum agents, or anti-metabolites. Clinical trial data (ECOG 1199 and other large adjuvant taxane trials) do not show increased distant recurrence with taxane-containing regimens, raising the question of whether the mouse model finding translates to clinical reality.

### 2.4 Bidirectional Feedback Loops

DTCs are not passive recipients of macrophage signals. Self-reinforcing circuits exist:

1. **TGF-beta amplification loop:** Macrophage-derived TGF-beta induces DTC dormancy, which feeds back to maintain an immunosuppressive niche favoring M2-like (anti-inflammatory, tissue-repair-associated) macrophage persistence.

2. **Epidermal Growth Factor (EGF)/Colony Stimulating Factor 1 (CSF-1) paracrine loop:** DTCs secrete CSF-1 to recruit and polarize macrophages, which reciprocate with EGF, reinforcing protumor signaling.

**CAVEATS:** These loops have not been formally modeled in the dormancy context. Their relative contribution to dormancy maintenance versus exit is unknown. Whether these loops operate identically across organ niches is undetermined. The extent to which cancer cell autonomous (autocrine) CSF-1/EGF signaling may bypass the paracrine loop has not been resolved.

### 2.5 The DDR1/STAT1/Type III Collagen Pathway

Tumor-derived Type III collagen constructs a pro-dormancy Extracellular Matrix (ECM) niche. Discoidin Domain Receptor 1 (DDR1) on DTCs is activated by this collagen, triggering STAT1-mediated dormancy gene expression and forming a positive feedback loop (Di Martino et al. 2022, confirmed). Macrophage-derived Matrix Metalloproteinases (MMPs) can degrade this collagen scaffold to enable dormancy exit.

**ESTABLISHED:** The DDR1/STAT1/collagen III pathway is confirmed by Di Martino et al. 2022 (PMID 35121989).

**CAVEATS:** The specific macrophage-derived signals that interact with the collagen III scaffold, and the conditions under which macrophages produce MMPs that degrade it versus maintain it, are incompletely characterized.

### 2.6 Epigenetic Regulation of Macrophage Polarization

Zheng et al. (2023, confirmed, published in Cancer Research) demonstrated that the Histone Deacetylase 2 (HDAC2)-Specificity Protein 1 (SP1) axis locks macrophages into a protumor (M2-like) state via histone H3 acetylation and SP1 transcriptional control. Class I Histone Deacetylase Inhibitors (HDACi) can disrupt this axis, inducing durable repolarization.

DNA Methyltransferase 3 Alpha (DNMT3a)-mediated DNA methylation has been implicated in macrophage polarization in atherosclerosis (Li et al. 2020, FASEB J, PMID 32052888) and hepatic fibrosis (Cui et al. 2022, Eur J Pharmacol, PMID 35597264). The specific claim that DNMT3a epigenetically locks macrophage polarization in the context of DTC dormancy has no identifiable source. The general principle is supported, but the specific dormancy-niche application is inferred rather than demonstrated.

### 2.7 Macrophage-Derived Exosomes

Walker et al. (2019, confirmed) reported that exosomes from in vitro-polarized M2-like macrophages maintain dormancy via Gap Junctional Intercellular Communication (GJIC), while M1-like macrophage exosomes break dormancy via Nuclear Factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells (NF-kB) signaling.

**CRITICAL CAVEATS:**
- This is a single in vitro study using breast cancer cell lines.
- GJIC requires specific connexin expression on both donor and recipient cells; whether dormant DTCs in vivo express the complementary connexins is undetermined.
- The M1/M2 exosome distinction is itself binary and may oversimplify what in vivo macrophage-derived exosomes actually carry.
- No independent group has replicated the GJIC-mediated dormancy maintenance finding in vivo.
- Exosome characterization standards (per International Society for Extracellular Vesicles MISEV2018/2023 guidelines) have not been discussed in the context of this study.

## SECTION 3: ORGAN-SPECIFIC NICHE BIOLOGY

### 3.1 Lung Niche

**Evidence Strength: STRONGEST**

Alveolar macrophages (tissue-resident, not monocyte-derived) act as dormancy gatekeepers via TGF-beta2/TGF-betaRIII signaling (Dalla et al. 2024, confirmed). This was demonstrated using CD169-Diphtheria Toxin Receptor (CD169-DTR) mice for specific alveolar macrophage depletion, combined with intravital imaging and clodronate controls.

**CAVEATS:**
- One organ, one cancer type (breast), one mouse model (HER2/neu transgenic with CD169-DTR system).
- The HER2/neu model is hormone-driven and immunogenic. Whether the same macrophage-dormancy axis operates in hormone receptor-negative or triple-negative breast cancer, or in non-breast cancers, is unknown.
- The study examines dormancy over weeks to months. Clinical dormancy can last decades.
- Clodronate liposomes deplete all phagocytic cells, not exclusively macrophages. The CD169-DTR system is more specific but CD169 is also expressed on marginal zone macrophages in the spleen and some dendritic cell subsets.

### 3.2 Bone Marrow Niche

**Evidence Strength: MODERATE (largely inferential)**

Osteomacs (bone marrow macrophages) and C-X-C Motif Chemokine Ligand 12 (CXCL12)/C-X-C Chemokine Receptor Type 4 (CXCR4) signaling create a dormancy-permissive niche. CXCL12 (also known as Stromal Cell-Derived Factor 1 [SDF-1]) is produced by osteoblasts and stromal cells; CXCR4 on DTCs mediates homing and retention.

**CAVEATS:**
- The bone marrow dormancy story relies heavily on review articles rather than primary macrophage-specific experimental data.
- Where is the primary data showing that bone marrow macrophage depletion or repolarization directly alters dormancy? This experiment has not been definitively performed.
- The CXCL12/CXCR4 axis is a stromal/osteoblast mechanism; macrophages may be supporting actors rather than protagonists.

### 3.3 Liver Niche

**Evidence Strength: MODERATE (macrophage role is indirect)**

Kupffer cells (liver-resident macrophages) engage the Hepatic Stellate Cell (HSC) -- Natural Killer (NK) cell axis (Correia et al. 2021, confirmed). The central finding of this paper is that HSCs suppress NK cell-sustained breast cancer dormancy. Kupffer cells interact with HSCs but are not demonstrated as the central regulator.

**CAVEATS:** Macrophages are mentioned as interacting with HSCs but the paper's mechanistic emphasis is on the HSC-NK axis, not macrophage centrality.

### 3.4 Brain Niche

**Evidence Strength: WEAK (least characterized)**

Microglia (Central Nervous System [CNS]-resident macrophages) and astrocytic laminin-211 (an ECM protein: laminin alpha-2 beta-1 gamma-1)/YAP signaling may modulate dormancy. The brain niche story (Dai et al. 2022, Nature Cancer) is primarily about astrocyte-derived laminin-211 signaling through dystroglycan/YAP. Microglia are barely discussed in the primary literature.

**CAVEATS:** No primary research demonstrates macrophage-mediated dormancy regulation in brain metastasis. This is the least characterized organ niche in the dormancy context.

### 3.6 Missing Organ Niches

Peritoneal and adrenal niches are recognized sites of DTC dormancy in ovarian and breast cancer respectively. Their omission from this analysis is acknowledged as a gap.

### 3.7 Niche Plasticity

Niches are not static. They are remodeled by tumor-derived signals, systemic inflammation, and therapeutic interventions. The concept of "niche flipping" -- where a dormancy-maintaining niche is converted to a permissive niche by macrophage repolarization, ECM remodeling, or inflammatory insult -- is implied by the data but not formally developed. Watson et al. 2024 (confirmed, in glioblastoma) demonstrated that fibrotic compensation after macrophage depletion creates new pro-metastatic niches, illustrating niche plasticity.

**SPECULATIVE:** Whether similar compensatory mechanisms operate in breast cancer dormancy niches is unknown.

## SECTION 4: DORMANCY EXIT AND REACTIVATION MECHANISMS

### 4.1 Inflammation-Driven Awakening

Pereira et al. (2025, confirmed) demonstrated that inflammatory cytokines mediate both the induction of and awakening from metastatic dormancy. The same pathways (p38 activation, STAT1 signaling) that induce dormancy can, in different temporal and intensity contexts, trigger awakening. This duality underscores the context-dependency of cytokine signaling in dormancy.

### 4.2 Chemotherapy-Induced Awakening (Taxane-Specific)

Ganesan et al. (2023, confirmed) demonstrated that taxane chemotherapy (specifically docetaxel) induces stromal injury in the bone marrow, leading to IL-6 and G-CSF release, which activates MEK/ERK signaling and breaks dormancy. This was demonstrated in the D2.0R/D2A1 murine dormancy model.

**IMPORTANT QUALIFICATIONS:**
- This finding is specific to the taxane class of chemotherapy. It has NOT been demonstrated for anthracyclines, platinum agents, or anti-metabolites.
- The in vivo evidence is mouse-only. Clinical evidence that adjuvant taxanes increase metastatic recurrence through dormancy exit is not available.
- Large adjuvant taxane trials (ECOG 1199 and others) showed either benefit or neutral effect on distant recurrence-free survival. The team did not observe increased distant recurrence with taxane-containing regimens. This discrepancy between the mouse model and clinical data must be acknowledged.
- The mechanism identified by Ganesan et al. is stromal injury leading to cytokine release, not macrophage polarization per se. The increase in M2-like macrophages may be a response to tissue damage rather than a cause of dormancy exit.

### 4.3 Neutrophil Extracellular Trap (NET)-TAM Synergy

The concept of Neutrophil Extracellular Trap (NET)-TAM synergy for dormancy exit is biologically plausible. NETs are web-like structures of DNA, histones, and granule proteins released by neutrophils that trap DTCs in pro-inflammatory microenvironments and may synergize with TAMs to promote reactivation.

**CAVEATS:** The specific citation originally supporting this concept (He et al. 2026) has been removed as unverifiable. The NET-TAM synergy concept is retained as hypothesis only. No verified primary citation directly demonstrates NET-TAM synergy in dormancy exit. Whether dormant DTCs actively suppress NET formation or neutrophil recruitment (a bidirectional consideration) has not been addressed.

### 4.4 TGF-beta1/NRP2 Axis in Dormancy Exit

Recalde-Percaz et al. (2025, confirmed) established that macrophage-derived TGF-beta1 promotes DTC awakening via NRP2 upregulation. This represents a specific isoform- and receptor-dependent mechanism distinct from the TGF-beta2/TGF-betaRIII dormancy maintenance pathway.

### 4.5 Pro-Resolving Macrophage Loss and Fibrotic-Like Niche Formation

Gilon-Zaltsman et al. (2025, confirmed) characterized the Fibrotic-Like Niche (FLN) model, in which loss of pro-resolving macrophages enables the establishment of a fibrotic tissue microenvironment that serves as a permissive niche for DTC reactivation.

**CAVEATS:** The specific receptors, effector pathways, and expected pharmacodynamic markers for pro-resolving macrophage function in the dormancy context have not been fully characterized.

### 4.6 The Surgical Vulnerability Window

A post-surgical vulnerability window (approximately 1 week to 6 months post-resection) during which dormant DTCs may be reactivated has been proposed. The concept is based on the biological plausibility that tissue injury and wound healing responses during surgery create a temporary window of macrophage dysregulation, with an initial immunosuppressive phase (wound healing) followed by immune restoration.

**IMPORTANT QUALIFICATIONS:**
- The primary citation (Tang et al. 2020) could not be independently verified during the citation audit and is flagged as UNVERIFIABLE.
- Even if the citation is valid, the specific vulnerability window timeline (Days 0-7: "peak immunosuppression," Days 7-14: "immune restoration begins") is presented with false precision.
- The window is unlikely to be uniform across bone marrow, lung, liver, and brain metastatic sites.
- The specific macrophage polarization changes during the perioperative period that contribute to dormancy exit have not been characterized.
- This concept should be treated as hypothesis, not established fact.

### 4.7 Metabolic Reprogramming

Metabolic switches (glycolysis vs. Oxidative Phosphorylation [OXPHOS], itaconate, succinate, arginine metabolism via Inducible Nitric Oxide Synthase [iNOS] vs. Arginase 1 [ARG1]) integrate with Hypoxia-Inducible Factor 1-alpha (HIF-1alpha)-driven M2-like polarization. Metabolic reprogramming is an under-explored dimension of macrophage-DTC crosstalk.

**CAVEATS:** Causal claims about metabolic reprogramming in dormancy are made from correlative data. The temporal dynamics of metabolic shifts during dormancy exit are unstudied.

## SECTION 4.8: ESTIMATION — M1, M2, OR BOTH: WHICH PHENOTYPE DRIVES DORMANCY ENTRY AND EXIT?

This section synthesizes evidence from all team reports to estimate the relative contribution of M1-like (classically activated, pro-inflammatory) and M2-like (alternatively activated, immunosuppressive) macrophage phenotypes to both **entry into** and **exit from** the dormant state. As established in Section 2, the M1/M2 binary is an acknowledged oversimplification — macrophage activation exists on a multidimensional continuum. However, evaluating the evidence through this binary lens remains heuristically valuable because: (a) most experimental interventions target one pole or the other, and (b) no dormancy-specific macrophage subset has been identified at the single-cell level to replace this framework.

### 4.8.1 Evidence for M1-Like Macrophages in Dormancy Entry

**Strength of evidence: MODERATE-STRONG**

| Evidence | Source | Implication |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| Interferon gamma (IFN-gamma, an M1-associated cytokine) directly induces dormancy in DTCs via high p38/ERK ratio and STAT1 activation | Pereira et al. 2025, Cell Reports (CONFIRMED) | M1-like signaling can drive dormancy entry |
| Tumor Necrosis Factor alpha (TNF-alpha, M1-associated) at low sustained levels promotes dormancy-maintenance signaling without triggering proliferation | Chernosky and Tamagno 2021 (reviewed in Vasquez briefing) | M1 cytokines contribute to dormancy |
| CXCL10/CXCR3 axis (produced by M1-like dendritic cells and macrophages) sustains immunological dormancy in TNBC | Yilmaz et al. 2026, Nature Breast Cancer | M1-derived chemokines maintain dormancy |
| CD8+ T cell-derived IFN-gamma induces dormancy; maintenance is T-cell independent | Pereira et al. 2025 | Innate-adaptive crosstalk where adaptive M1-like signals initiate, but maintenance may be handed off |

**ESTIMATION: M1-like macrophages contribute to dormancy entry with MODERATE-STRONG confidence.** The IFN-gamma/p38/STAT1 axis is a well-characterized mechanistic pathway. However, the in vivo evidence that M1-like macrophages themselves (rather than CD8+ T cells or NK cells producing IFN-gamma) are the primary source of dormancy-inducing IFN-gamma is less direct. M1 macrophages are contributors, not sole agents.

### 4.8.2 Evidence for M2-Like Macrophages in Dormancy Entry

**Strength of evidence: MODERATE**

| Evidence | Source | Implication |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| M2-like macrophage exosomes maintain dormancy via Gap Junctional Intercellular Communication (GJIC) in bone marrow stroma | Walker et al. 2019, Cell Death and Disease (CONFIRMED WITH CAVEAT) | M2 exosomes enforce dormancy |
| Alveolar macrophages (tissue-resident, with M2-like characteristics including TGF-beta2 secretion) maintain lung DTC dormancy | Dalla et al. 2024, Cell (CONFIRMED) | M2-like resident macrophages are dormancy gatekeepers |
| TGF-beta2 (canonical M2 cytokine) through TGF-betaRIII enforces dormancy via p38 activation and NR2F1 upregulation | Dalla et al. 2024; Zhang et al. 2025 (CONFIRMED) | M2-derived TGF-beta2 is a dormancy maintainer |
| M2-like macrophages in primary tumors prime DTCs for both dissemination AND dormancy (NR2F1+, SOX9+) | Borriello et al. 2022, Nature Communications (CONFIRMED) | M2 macrophages install dormancy programs pre-dissemination |
| Interleukin-10 (IL-10, M2 cytokine) creates immune-permissive environment allowing dormant DTC survival | Multiple reviews (confirmed) | M2 signaling creates survival niche |

**ESTIMATION: M2-like macrophages contribute to dormancy entry and maintenance with MODERATE confidence.** The evidence is strongest for tissue-resident M2-like macrophages (alveolar macrophages in lung, osteomacs in bone) as dormancy gatekeepers. The Borriello et al. finding that primary tumor macrophages pre-install dormancy programs is particularly compelling but does not distinguish whether the priming macrophages are M1-like or M2-like at the single-cell level.

### 4.8.3 Evidence for M1-Like Macrophages in Dormancy Exit

**Strength of evidence: MODERATE**

| Evidence | Source | Implication |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| M1-like exosomes (containing NF-kappaB-activating cargo) break dormancy in breast cancer cells | Walker et al. 2019 (CONFIRMED WITH CAVEAT) | M1 exosomes can awaken dormant DTCs |
| TLR4 activation switches M2 macrophages to M1, which then produce NF-kappaB-activating exosomes that break dormancy | Walker et al. 2019 | M1 repolarization → dormancy exit |
| Inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, IL-6, associated with M1 activation) can activate NF-kappaB in DTCs, driving cell cycle re-entry | Zhang et al. 2025 PNAS; Multiple reviews | M1 inflammatory milieu promotes awakening |
| Chemotherapy-induced IL-6 (from stromal injury, activating M1-like inflammatory response) triggers MEK/ERK and dormancy exit | Ganesan et al. 2023 (CONFIRMED) | M1-associated inflammation contributes to awakening |
| IL-17A from CD4+ T cells (amplified by M1 macrophage inflammatory signaling) is the essential wake-up signal | Pereira et al. 2025 (CONFIRMED) | M1-amplified adaptive immunity drives exit |

**ESTIMATION: M1-like macrophages contribute to dormancy exit with MODERATE confidence.** The Walker et al. exosome study provides direct mechanistic evidence, but it is an in vitro system. The inflammatory awakening pathway (NF-kappaB, IL-6/ERK) is well-characterized but the specific contribution of macrophage-derived (vs. stromal or T cell-derived) inflammatory signals has not been disambiguated in vivo.

### 4.8.4 Evidence for M2-Like Macrophages in Dormancy Exit

**Strength of evidence: MODERATE (paradoxical — M2 drives both entry AND exit)**

| Evidence | Source | Implication |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| M2-like TAMs produce Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor (VEGF), basic Fibroblast Growth Factor (bFGF), and angiopoietin-2, driving the angiogenic switch that enables outgrowth | Multiple reviews (confirmed) | M2 signaling creates conditions for eventual exit |
| TGF-beta1 (produced by M2-like macrophages) upregulates NRP2 on DTCs, promoting proliferation and invasion | Recalde-Percaz et al. 2025 (CONFIRMED) | M2-derived TGF-beta1 isoform drives exit |
| M2-like TAMs produce Matrix Metalloproteinases (MMP-9, MMP-13) that degrade Type III collagen dormancy scaffold | Di Martino et al. 2022 (CONFIRMED, mechanistic link inferred) | M2 ECM remodeling enables escape |
| M2-like macrophages recruit regulatory T cells (Tregs) via CCL22 and suppress NK cell function via TGF-beta and Prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) | Multiple reviews (confirmed) | M2 immunosuppression protects reawakening DTCs from clearance |
| Taxane-induced M2 polarization increase in bone marrow correlates with dormancy escape | Ganesan et al. 2023 (CONFIRMED) | Shift toward M2 dominance accompanies awakening |
| M2-like TAMs secrete EGF activating EGFR on tumor cells, promoting proliferative signaling | Friedman-DeLuca et al. 2024 (CONFIRMED) | M2 paracrine signaling drives proliferation |

**ESTIMATION: M2-like macrophages contribute to dormancy exit with MODERATE confidence.** This is paradoxical: the same M2-like phenotype that enforces dormancy (via TGF-beta2, GJIC) also creates conditions for eventual exit (via VEGF, MMPs, NRP2, immunosuppression). The resolution likely lies in temporal dynamics: early M2 dominance sustains dormancy, but chronic M2 polarization eventually creates a pro-outgrowth niche through angiogenic switching and immune evasion.

### 4.8.5 Integrated Estimation: The Dual-Role Model

**CONCLUSION: BOTH M1-like AND M2-like macrophages contribute to BOTH dormancy entry and exit, but through distinct mechanisms and with different temporal profiles.**

The evidence supports the following model:

**DORMANCY ENTRY (induction and early maintenance):**
- **M1 contribution (MODERATE-STRONG):** IFN-gamma/p38/STAT1 axis directly induces growth arrest; CXCL10/CXCR3 sustains immunological dormancy
- **M2 contribution (MODERATE):** TGF-beta2/TGF-betaRIII enforces quiescence; M2 exosomes deliver dormancy-maintaining miRNAs via GJIC; M2-mediated immunosuppression creates niche for DTC survival
- **Net assessment: Both phenotypes cooperate to install and enforce dormancy.** The M1 axis provides the initial growth arrest signal (IFN-gamma), while the M2 axis provides the sustained niche maintenance (TGF-beta2, GJIC). Neither alone is sufficient in most models.

**DORMANCY EXIT (reactivation and outgrowth):**
- **M1 contribution (MODERATE):** NF-kappaB-activating exosomes break dormancy; inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, IL-6) shift ERK/p38 ratio toward proliferation; IL-17A amplification
- **M2 contribution (MODERATE):** TGF-beta1/NRP2 axis drives proliferative reawakening; angiogenic switch (VEGF, angiopoietin-2); ECM degradation (MMP-9); immune evasion (Treg recruitment, NK suppression)
- **Net assessment: Both phenotypes can independently drive exit, but through fundamentally different mechanisms.** M1-driven exit is inflammatory and rapid (NF-kappaB activation, cytokine surge). M2-driven exit is permissive and gradual (angiogenic switching, ECM remodeling, immune evasion). In vivo, both likely operate simultaneously during the exit transition.

### 4.8.6 The Temporal Switch Model

The synthesis suggests a **temporal switch model** rather than a static M1/M2 assignment:

```
PRIMARY TUMOR          DISSEMINATION          EARLY NICHE          LATE NICHE          EXIT
     │                     │                    │                    │                  │
     ▼                     ▼                    ▼                    ▼                  ▼
 TMEM macrophages     DTCs arrive with     M1-like IFN-gamma   M2-like TGF-beta2  Perturbation:
 prime DTCs for       dormancy programs    induces entry;      maintains long-    inflammation,
 BOTH dissemination   (NR2F1+, SOX9+)      M2-like TGF-beta2   term dormancy      hypoxia, therapy
 AND dormancy         pre-installed         sustains quiescence  via GJIC           shifts balance
 (Borriello 2022)                           (Dalla 2024)                           (Ganesan 2023,
                                                                            Recalde-Percaz 2025)
```

**Phase 1 (Primary tumor):** Macrophages at TMEM doorways prime DTCs for dormancy. The priming macrophage subset has not been classified as M1 or M2 (Borriello et al. 2022 did not perform single-cell phenotyping).

**Phase 2 (Early seeding):** M1-like IFN-gamma signaling induces initial growth arrest (Pereira et al. 2025). This is an active, immune-mediated process.

**Phase 3 (Dormancy maintenance, months to decades):** M2-like tissue-resident macrophages (alveolar macrophages, osteomacs) sustain dormancy through TGF-beta2, GJIC exosomes, and CXCL12/CXCR4 signaling. The M1 contribution diminishes as the adaptive immune response contracts.

**Phase 4 (Exit trigger):** A perturbation (inflammation, therapy, tissue injury, aging) shifts the macrophage balance. This can manifest as:
- **M1-driven exit:** Inflammatory surge (NF-kappaB, IL-6, TNF-alpha) rapidly shifts ERK/p38 ratio
- **M2-driven exit:** Gradual angiogenic switch, ECM remodeling, and immune evasion enable slow outgrowth
- **Combined exit:** Most likely in vivo — an inflammatory trigger (M1) initiates awakening, followed by M2-mediated angiogenesis and immune evasion that support outgrowth

### 4.8.7 Quantitative Estimation

Given the limitations of the evidence, a tentative quantitative estimation of phenotype contribution:

| Process | M1 Contribution | M2 Contribution | Other (stromal, T cells, NK) | Confidence |
|---------|:--------------:|:---------------:|:----------------------------:|:----------:|
| Dormancy entry (induction) | **40%** (IFN-gamma, p38/STAT1) | **25%** (TGF-beta2, GJIC priming) | **35%** (CD8+ T cells, NK cells, stromal signals) | LOW |
| Dormancy maintenance (long-term) | **15%** (CXCL10, residual IFN-gamma) | **50%** (TGF-beta2, GJIC, niche architecture) | **35%** (ECM scaffolding, stromal cells, NK surveillance) | LOW-MODERATE |
| Dormancy exit (reactivation) | **35%** (NF-kappaB, inflammatory cytokines, IL-17A amplification) | **35%** (angiogenic switch, MMP/ECM, TGF-beta1/NRP2, immune evasion) | **30%** (fibroblast activation, NETs, direct stromal injury) | LOW |

**CRITICAL CAVEAT:** These percentages are **estimates based on the relative weight of mechanistic evidence**, not quantitative measurements. No study has performed the definitive experiment: tracking individual macrophages of defined polarization state interacting with individual DTCs through the full dormancy lifecycle in vivo. The percentages are provided to communicate the team's assessment of relative contribution, not as empirical values. They should not be cited as data.

### 4.8.8 Key Unresolved Questions

1. **What triggers the transition from Phase 3 (M2-maintained dormancy) to Phase 4 (exit)?** The perturbation may be extrinsic (inflammation, therapy) or intrinsic (macrophage aging, metabolic exhaustion). No longitudinal tracking data exist.

2. **Are the "dormancy-maintaining M2 macrophages" and the "exit-promoting M2 macrophages" the same cells at different time points, or distinct subpopulations?** If they are the same cells, the temporal switch is stochastic. If they are distinct subpopulations, the shift represents a change in niche composition (e.g., recruitment of monocyte-derived macrophages replacing tissue-resident macrophages, as suggested by Dalla et al. 2024).

3. **Can the M1 inflammatory exit be therapeutically leveraged for immune clearance?** If M1-driven awakening is followed by efficient immune elimination of DTCs, the strategy of deliberate inflammatory awakening followed by checkpoint blockade may be viable. If awakening outpaces clearance, the strategy is dangerous. This balance has never been quantified.

4. **Is the "M2-maintained dormancy" phase actually a state of immune-evasive quiescence that the immune system cannot detect?** Goddard et al. (2024, Cancer Cell, confirmed) demonstrated that dormant DTCs evade immunity primarily through their scarcity, not active immune suppression. If true, then M2-mediated immune suppression may be irrelevant during single-cell dormancy and only become important during micrometastatic (oligoclonal) outgrowth.

## SECTION 5: THERAPEUTIC LANDSCAPE ASSESSMENT

**PREAMBLE:** No macrophage-targeting therapy has been evaluated in any preclinical model or clinical trial with dormancy-specific endpoints (time to Minimal Residual Disease [MRD] detection, circulating tumor cell dormancy markers, or organ-specific metastatic recurrence prevention). All therapeutic assessments in this section are hypothesis-generating extrapolations from advanced-disease trial data and mechanism-of-action reasoning. They do not constitute clinical recommendations.

### 5.1 CSF-1R Inhibitors (Pexidartinib, Emactuzumab, Cabiralizumab)

**Mechanism:** Blockade of the Colony Stimulating Factor 1 Receptor (CSF-1R, also known as CD115) depletes macrophages by abrogating survival and differentiation signals. Pexidartinib is FDA-approved for Tenosynovial Giant Cell Tumor (TGCT).

**Dormancy risk assessment: HIGH awakening risk (theoretical).** Indiscriminate TAM depletion may remove dormancy-enforcing macrophage populations. Watson et al. 2024 (confirmed, glioblastoma) demonstrated that fibrotic responses to macrophage depletion create new pro-metastatic niches. Whether this applies to breast cancer dormancy niches is untested.

**Clinical evidence:** Pexidartinib achieved more than 80% TAM depletion in on-treatment biopsies but has limited single-agent activity in solid tumors. Voissiere et al. 2024 (Science Translational Medicine) demonstrated that pexidartinib impairs FLT3-dependent dendritic cell differentiation.

**Assessment:** Risky as monotherapy in dormancy context. The depletion paradox is unresolved.

### 5.2 PI3K-gamma Inhibitors (Eganelisib / IPI-549)

**Mechanism:** Phosphoinositide 3-Kinase Gamma (PI3K-gamma) is predominantly expressed in myeloid cells. Inhibition repolarizes TAMs without depleting them.

**Clinical evidence:** MARIO-1 Phase 1/1b (Hong et al. 2023, confirmed) showed manageable safety and antitumor activity. MARIO-3 Phase 2 (O'Connell et al. 2024, confirmed) in frontline metastatic Triple-Negative Breast Cancer (TNBC) showed TAM reprogramming, immune activation, and ECM reorganization.

**Dormancy risk assessment: MODERATE (theoretical -- most favorable risk-benefit profile among available agents).** Repolarization rather than depletion is theoretically preferable. ECM reorganization data from MARIO-3 are relevant to dormancy niches.

**CRITICAL UNRESOLVED QUESTIONS:**
- Does PI3K-gamma inhibition preferentially repolarize pro-outgrowth macrophages while sparing dormancy-maintaining signals? Unknown.
- Could M2-to-M1 repolarization eliminate tissue-resident macrophage populations that maintain dormancy through non-inflammatory mechanisms (e.g., alveolar macrophage TGF-betaRIII signaling)? Unaddressed.
- PI3K-gamma inhibition affects not only macrophages but also neutrophils, Myeloid-Derived Suppressor Cells (MDSCs), and T cells (O'Connell et al. 2024). The net effect on dormancy niches is unpredictable.
- Eganelisib's developer (Infinity Pharmaceuticals) has an unclear operational status, raising questions about drug availability for future trials.

**Assessment:** Candidate warranting dormancy-specific preclinical testing. Not a "lead therapeutic candidate" in the clinical sense.

### 5.3 STING Agonists

**Mechanism:** Activation of the cyclic GMP-AMP Synthase (cGAS)-Stimulator of Interferon Genes (STING) pathway promotes Type I interferon production, dendritic cell maturation, and innate immune activation.

**Dormancy risk assessment: HIGH awakening risk (theoretical), HIGH clearance potential (theoretical).** STING activation generates a potent inflammatory milieu that could break dormancy via NF-kB and inflammatory cytokine signaling. If immune-mediated clearance is efficient, the net effect may be favorable.

**Assessment:** High-risk, high-reward strategy. The risk-benefit calculus depends on the balance between awakening and immediate immune elimination of DTCs -- a balance that has not been experimentally quantified.

### 5.4 CD40 Agonists

**Mechanism:** CD40 activation converts TAMs from M2-like immunosuppressive to M1-like tumoricidal phenotypes, licenses dendritic cells, and primes tumor-specific T cells. McVey and Beatty (2025, confirmed) provided a definitive review.

**Dormancy risk assessment: HIGH awakening risk (theoretical), LOW-MODERATE clearance potential (theoretical).**

**Assessment:** Acceptable only with effective clearance partner. Whether awakened DTCs are efficiently cleared depends on T-cell fitness in the metastatic niche, which is often immunosuppressive.

### 5.5 HDAC Inhibitors (Class I: Entinostat, Mocetinostat)

**Mechanism:** The HDAC2-SP1 axis (Zheng et al. 2023, confirmed, Cancer Research) locks macrophages into a protumor state. Class I HDAC inhibitors disrupt this axis.

**Dormancy risk assessment: MODERATE for both awakening and clearance (theoretical).** Epigenetic reprogramming may be slower-acting than inflammatory repolarization, potentially offering more controlled disruption of the dormancy niche.

**Assessment:** Promising but needs dormancy-specific preclinical testing.

### 5.6 Risk-Benefit Framework (Theoretical)

| Agent | Awakening Risk (Theoretical) | Clearance Potential (Theoretical) | Net Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| CSF1R inhibitor | HIGH | MODERATE | Risky; depletion paradox unresolved |
| PI3K-gamma inhibitor | MODERATE | LOW-MODERATE | Most favorable theoretical profile |
| STING agonist | HIGH | HIGH | High-risk, high-reward; balance unquantified |
| CD40 agonist | HIGH | LOW-MODERATE | Requires effective clearance partner |
| HDAC inhibitor | MODERATE | MODERATE | Promising; needs preclinical testing |

**MANDATORY CAVEAT:** This framework is based on mechanism-of-action reasoning only. No dormancy-specific empirical data are available for any agent. The distinction between "depletion" and "repolarization" is presented as clean but is not: PI3K-gamma inhibition also affects neutrophils, MDSCs, and T cells. If macrophage repolarization triggers compensatory fibrosis (as macrophage depletion does in glioblastoma), the risk profile may be higher than estimated. The framework is an informed opinion, not a data-driven assessment.

### 5.7 Biomarker Considerations

**CD68/CD163 Ratio:** Darvishian et al. 2022 (confirmed) reported HR 10.32 for DCIS recurrence prediction. **CRITICAL LIMITATIONS:** N=67 patients, 13 recurrence events, 95% CI 1.34-80.0. This is a preliminary finding in a tiny cohort with a confidence interval spanning two orders of magnitude. It has not been independently validated. The study was in DCIS (not invasive cancer with dormant DTCs). This is NOT a validated biomarker.

**TMEM Doorway Scores:** Parmar et al. 2026 (confirmed) reported TMEM scores associated with distant recurrence across breast cancer subtypes. This is the stronger biomarker candidate but has not been validated specifically for dormancy applications.

**No validated clinical dormancy biomarker exists.** Without the ability to identify which patients harbor dormant DTCs and in what state, clinical trial design for dormancy-specific endpoints relies on surrogate markers that have not been validated for this purpose.

## SECTION 6: CRITICAL LIMITATIONS AND CAVEATS

This section incorporates all criticisms raised by the adversarial reviewer (Richter) and the Chief Scientist (Garcia-Lopez).

### 6.1 The Temporal Scaling Problem

All mechanistic claims in this synthesis are based on mouse models or in vitro systems. Mouse dormancy models (D2.0R/D2A1, HER2/neu transgenic, PyMT) model dormancy over weeks to months. Human clinical dormancy lasts years to decades. The molecular mechanisms that maintain quiescence for 3 weeks in a mouse may not be the same mechanisms that maintain quiescence for 15 years in a patient. The temporal scaling problem is unaddressed in the literature and represents a fundamental limitation on the translational applicability of all findings in this document.

### 6.2 In Vitro vs. In Vivo Distinction

Major findings exist on a spectrum of evidentiary strength:

**In vivo confirmed (specific organ/model):**
- Alveolar macrophage dormancy maintenance in lung (Dalla et al. 2024)
- Taxane-induced stromal injury and dormancy escape (Ganesan et al. 2023)

**In vitro only (not confirmed in vivo):**
- M2 exosome dormancy maintenance via GJIC (Walker et al. 2019)
- HDAC2-SP1 macrophage polarization locking (Zheng et al. 2023 -- the dormancy-specific application is in vitro)
- Most cytokine signaling data

**Inferred from other disease contexts:**
- DNMT3a epigenetic locking in dormancy (from atherosclerosis/hepatic fibrosis)
- Fibrotic compensation (from glioblastoma; Watson et al. 2024)

### 6.3 Model System Limitations

- **D2.0R/D2A1 murine system:** Selected for dormancy phenotype (potential selection artifact). Does not represent the heterogeneity of human breast cancer subtypes.
- **HER2/neu transgenic model:** Hormone-driven and immunogenic. May not represent hormone receptor-negative or triple-negative breast cancer.
- **Clodronate liposomes:** Deplete all phagocytic cells (dendritic cells, monocytes, other phagocytes), not exclusively macrophages.
- **CD169-DTR system:** More specific but CD169 is also expressed on splenic marginal zone macrophages and some dendritic cell subsets.

### 6.4 Absence of Human Dormancy Data

Every mechanistic claim in this synthesis is based on mouse models or in vitro systems. Zero human studies have validated any of these mechanisms in the context of dormant DTCs. The closest human data are:
- Darvishian et al. 2022: 67-patient DCIS study with 13 events (not dormant DTCs)
- Parmar et al. 2026: TMEM scoring for recurrence risk (correlative, not mechanistic)

### 6.5 Cross-Organ Extrapolation Limitations

The lung niche has strong mechanistic evidence for macrophage centrality in dormancy. The team extrapolates to bone marrow, liver, and brain without comparable evidence. Each extrapolation is a significant limitation:
- Bone marrow: Relies on reviews; no primary macrophage depletion data
- Liver: Central mechanism is HSC-NK axis; macrophages are supporting actors
- Brain: No primary data for macrophage-mediated dormancy regulation

### 6.6 Statistical Limitations

- **Darvishian et al. 2022 HR 10.32:** 95% CI 1.34-80.0 (N=67, 13 events). The true hazard ratio could be as low as 1.34 (clinically insignificant) or as high as 80 (enormous). The point estimate is essentially meaningless with a CI spanning two orders of magnitude.
- **ERK/p38 ratios:** No standardized quantitative threshold for "dormant" vs. "proliferative" across experimental systems.
- **TAM depletion percentages:** Context-dependent and not directly comparable across studies.

### 6.7 The M1/M2 Binary Contradiction

This document acknowledges that the M1/M2 binary framework is an oversimplification of a multidimensional continuum. Single-cell RNA Sequencing (scRNA-seq) has identified multiple TAM subsets (Triggering Receptor Expressed on Myeloid Cells 2 [TREM2]+, Secreted Phosphoprotein 1 [SPP1]+, Folate Receptor Beta [FOLR2]+, Macrophage Receptor with Collagenous Structure [MARCO]+) that do not fit neatly into binary categories.

However, the therapeutic framework in Section 5 continues to use M1/M2 terminology because: (a) all available clinical data for macrophage-targeting agents are reported in M1/M2 terms; (b) no dormancy-specific macrophage subsets have been identified at the single-cell level; and (c) the functional terminology needed to replace M1/M2 in the dormancy context (i.e., "dormancy-promoting" vs. "dormancy-disrupting" subsets) cannot be defined until the relevant subsets are identified.

This is an acknowledged internal contradiction. The team cannot simultaneously reject and rely on the M1/M2 binary. This contradiction reflects the current state of the field, not an oversight.

### 6.8 Correlation vs. Causation

Throughout the synthesis, the co-localization of macrophages with dormant DTCs is presented as evidence that macrophages regulate dormancy. Dalla et al. (2024) provide the strongest causal evidence (macrophage depletion causes dormancy exit), but the other niche stories rely on correlative data. Spatial association does not establish regulatory function.

### 6.9 Alternative Interpretations of Key Findings

#### 6.9.1 Macrophages as Passive Bystanders
An alternative interpretation of Dalla et al. (2024) is that alveolar macrophage depletion disrupts the lung microenvironment in ways not specific to macrophage-DTC signaling. Dormant DTCs may re-enter the cell cycle because the niche is destabilized, not because a specific macrophage-derived signal was withdrawn.

#### 6.9.2 TGF-beta as Symptom, Not Cause
Elevated TGF-beta in dormancy niches could be a consequence of the quiescent state, not its cause. Dormant DTCs may create a quiescent, hypoxic niche that favors TGF-beta production by multiple cell types.

#### 6.9.3 Chemotherapy Awakening via Direct Stromal Toxicity
An equally valid interpretation of Ganesan et al. (2023) is that direct stromal injury -- independent of macrophages -- releases proliferative signals. The increase in M2 macrophages may be a response to tissue damage, not a cause of dormancy exit.

#### 6.9.4 CSF-1/EGF Loop as Cancer Cell Autonomous
Many cancer cells express both CSF-1 and Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor (EGFR) and can signal autocrinely. The extent to which dormancy regulation depends on the paracrine loop (requiring macrophages) vs. autocrine signaling is unresolved.

#### 6.9.5 Stromal Compensation Nullifying Interventions
If macrophage depletion or repolarization triggers compensatory fibrosis, inflammation, or recruitment of other immunosuppressive cells (MDSCs, Regulatory T cells [Tregs]), the net effect on dormant DTCs may be neutral or harmful regardless of the strategy chosen.

#### 6.9.6 Angiogenic Switch Independent of Macrophage Polarization
Angiogenic dormancy may be primarily regulated by endothelial cell-DTC interactions, with macrophage-derived angiogenic factors being contributory but not decisive.

### 6.10 Missing Evidence Inventory

1. **No dormancy-specific macrophage subset has been identified.** scRNA-seq studies profile TAMs in established tumors, not in dormant niches. No study has performed single-cell analysis of macrophages in direct physical contact with dormant vs. proliferative DTCs.
2. **No time-course data on macrophage polarization during DTC awakening.** Does macrophage repolarization precede, coincide with, or follow DTC awakening? If it follows, targeting macrophages will not prevent awakening.
3. **No human dormancy data for any mechanistic claim.** Every claim is based on mouse models or in vitro systems.
4. **No pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic data for macrophage-targeting agents in dormancy niches.** Whether eganelisib reaches bone marrow, lung alveoli, liver sinusoids, or brain parenchyma at concentrations sufficient to repolarize tissue-resident macrophages is unknown.
5. **No evidence comparing "maintain dormancy" vs. "awaken and eliminate" strategies.** The assumption that maintaining dormancy is preferred is untested.
6. **No cross-organ comparison of macrophage-dormancy mechanisms in the same experimental system.**
7. **No mathematical or computational model of the dormancy-macrophage interaction.** A systems biology approach could help predict outcomes of therapeutic interventions.
8. **No independent replication** of several key findings (Walker et al. 2019 GJIC mechanism; Ganesan et al. 2023 taxane awakening in other model systems).

## SECTION 8: PRIORITIZED RESEARCH AGENDA

The clinical development roadmap has been removed per the Richter critique and replaced with the following prioritized preclinical research agenda.

### Priority 1: Citation Integrity Remediation (IMMEDIATE -- 2 weeks)

All team members must re-verify every citation in their reports against PubMed primary sources. Origin of removed citations (He et al. 2026, Sun et al. 2023) must be investigated and documented as a research integrity matter.

### Priority 2: Identify Dormancy-Specific Macrophage Subsets (URGENT -- 6 months)

Perform spatial transcriptomics of macrophage-DTC pairs from dormant vs. reactivated niches. This is the single most important experiment in the field. Until dormancy-enforcing and dormancy-breaking macrophage subsets are identified at the single-cell level, all therapeutic strategies carry unpredictable risk.

Responsible: Dr. Okonkwo (organ biology), Dr. Patel (emerging mechanisms), Prof. Moretti (signaling framework)

### Priority 3: Dormancy-Specific Preclinical Testing of Eganelisib (URGENT -- 6 months)

Test eganelisib in established dormancy models (D2.0R/D2A1 murine system, patient-derived organoid dormancy assays). Endpoints: time to MRD detection, dormant DTC burden (quantitative bioluminescence), macrophage polarization state at the dormant niche (spatial transcriptomics).

Responsible: Dr. Chen (therapeutics), Prof. Klein (clinical translation)

### Priority 4: Resolve the PI3K-gamma Inhibition Contradiction (URGENT -- 4 months)

Produce a detailed mechanistic analysis of how PI3K-gamma inhibition affects dormancy-promoting vs. dormancy-disrupting macrophage subpopulations in each organ niche. Incorporate the Dalla et al. 2024 alveolar macrophage TGF-betaRIII data. Determine whether the eganelisib strategy should proceed, be modified for organ-specific application, or be abandoned.

Responsible: Dr. Chen (lead), Dr. Vasquez (signaling), Prof. Klein (therapeutic context)

### Priority 5: Unified Signaling Model and Terminological Reform (HIGH -- 6 months)

Produce a single, integrated signaling diagram reconciling all pathway inputs with organ-specific modifiers. Adopt functional terminology (dormancy-promoting, dormancy-permissive, dormancy-disrupting) to replace M1/M2 where possible. Identify specific macrophage subpopulations (TREM2+, MARCO+, CX3CR1+) relevant to each organ.

Responsible: Prof. Moretti (lead), Dr. Vasquez (pathway data), Dr. Okonkwo (organ-specific data)

### Priority 6: Temporal Dynamics of Macrophage Repolarization During DTC Awakening (HIGH -- 6 months)

Characterize whether macrophage polarization changes precede, coincide with, or follow DTC reactivation. If macrophage changes follow DTC awakening, targeting macrophages will not prevent dormancy exit.

Responsible: Dr. Patel (lead), Dr. Vasquez (signaling)

### Priority 7: Organ-Specific Therapeutic Vulnerability Analysis (HIGH -- 9 months)

Develop organ-specific therapeutic strategies based on the unique macrophage biology of each niche. For each organ, identify the most promising therapeutic target, expected mechanism, potential off-target effects, and candidate biomarkers.

Responsible: Dr. Okonkwo (organ biology), Dr. Chen (therapeutics), Prof. Klein (synthesis)

### Priority 8: Biomarker Validation (MODERATE -- 12 months)

Validate the CD68/CD163 ratio in a retrospective cohort of adjuvant breast cancer patients with adequate sample size (minimum 200 patients, 50+ events). Validate TMEM scores for dormancy-specific prediction. Commission formal statistical review of all biomarker claims.

Responsible: Prof. Klein (lead), Dr. Bergstrom (translational data)

### Priority 9: Perioperative Macrophage Dynamics Characterization (MODERATE -- 12 months)

Characterize specific changes in macrophage polarization during the perioperative period. Develop organ-specific vulnerability timelines.

Responsible: Dr. Bergstrom (lead), Dr. Okonkwo (organ-specific analysis)

### Priority 10: Bidirectional Signaling Loop Formalization (MODERATE -- 12 months)

Formalize bidirectional tumor-macrophage signaling loops into testable mathematical or systems biology models. Identify key nodes amenable to therapeutic intervention.

Responsible: Dr. Patel (lead), Dr. Vasquez (signaling), Prof. Moretti (modeling)
