# ADVERSARIAL PEER REVIEW AND CRITICAL APPRAISAL

**Reviewer:** Prof. Klaus Richter, PhD
**Affiliation:** Division of Macrophage Biology and Tumor Immunology, University of Heidelberg (emeritus); Former Editor, *European Journal of Immunology* (2008-2019)
**Date:** 26 May 2026
**Re:** Critical Review of the Integrated Research Synthesis on "Macrophage Polarization in Dormant Disseminated Tumor Cell Regulation" — Submitted by the Senior Scientist Team (Profs. Moretti and Klein), Incorporating Postdoctoral Briefings (Drs. Vasquez, Okonkwo, Chen, Patel, and Bergstrom)

---

## PREAMBLE

I have read the entire corpus: five postdoctoral briefings and two senior scientist integration reports. I will be direct. The effort is ambitious, the literature trawl is wide, and several genuinely important papers (Dalla et al. 2024, Borriello et al. 2022, Pereira et al. 2025) are correctly identified and discussed. But this synthesis suffers from three systemic problems that, if not addressed, will make it unpublishable and, worse, scientifically misleading.

**First**, the team repeatedly conflates correlation with causation. TGF-beta is elevated in dormancy niches. This does not mean TGF-beta *causes* or *maintains* dormancy in vivo. Most of the "mechanistic" evidence comes from in vitro systems or mouse models that are not validated for dormancy endpoints.

**Second**, the team has an alarming citation integrity problem. At least three citations are unverifiable or hallucinated, one is attributed to the wrong journal, and one of the clinical cornerstone studies (Darvishian et al. 2022) has a sample size so small that the headline hazard ratio is statistically meaningless. The team treats this HR 10.32 as robust evidence. It is not.

**Third**, the therapeutic roadmap is built on sand. The lead candidate (eganelisib) comes from a company (Infinity Pharmaceuticals) whose operational status is unclear, whose MARIO-3 trial was in advanced metastatic disease — not dormancy — and whose drug has never been tested in any dormancy-relevant model. The entire Phase 1-4 clinical development plan is an exercise in optimistic extrapolation from zero dormancy-specific data points.

I will now proceed through each claim systematically.

---

## SECTION 1: SYSTEMATIC WEAKNESS ANALYSIS

### Claim 1: "Macrophage polarization is a central rheostat governing DTC dormancy entry, maintenance, and escape"

**Grade: MODERATE**

The word "central" is doing enormous work here. The evidence that macrophages are *involved* in dormancy regulation is reasonable. The evidence that they are the *central* rheostat — rather than one of many niche components — is not established. Let me be specific:

- Dalla et al. (2024) demonstrated that alveolar macrophage depletion causes dormant DCCs to proliferate. This is a strong mechanistic result, but it was obtained in a single organ (lung), a single cancer type (breast), and a single mouse model (HER2/neu transgenic with CD169-DTR system). The claim of "central rheostat" requires demonstration across multiple organ systems and cancer types. The team provides no such evidence.

- The liver niche story (Correia et al. 2021, *Nature*) is about hepatic stellate cells suppressing NK cells. Macrophages (Kupffer cells) are mentioned as interacting with HSCs but are not demonstrated as the *central* regulator. The paper's central finding is the HSC-NK axis.

- The brain niche story (Dai et al. 2022, *Nature Cancer*) is about astrocyte-derived laminin-211 signaling through dystroglycan/YAP. Microglia are barely discussed. Moretti's own report lists the brain niche as the "least characterized" and admits there is "no primary research demonstrating macrophage-mediated dormancy regulation in brain metastasis."

- The bone marrow niche relies on osteoblast-derived CXCL12/CXCR4 signaling and osteomac function, which is largely inferred from review articles (Jin et al. 2025 is a review; Cassetta and Pollard 2023 is a review). Where is the primary data showing that bone marrow macrophage depletion or repolarization directly alters dormancy?

The team has taken evidence from one well-characterized organ (lung), supplemented it with tangential evidence from other organs where macrophages are supporting actors rather than protagonists, and declared macrophages the "central rheostat" across all niches. This is overreach.

**Recommendation:** Replace "central rheostat" with "a significant modulator." Acknowledge that in the lung niche specifically, the evidence for macrophage centrality is strong, but in other niches the evidence is incomplete, indirect, or absent.

### Claim 2: "The M1/M2 binary framework is insufficient; a spectrum model better captures macrophage diversity"

**Grade: STRONG (with caveats)**

This is the most defensible claim in the entire synthesis. The inadequacy of the M1/M2 binary has been established by multiple independent groups using scRNA-seq: TREM2+ TAMs (Coulton et al. 2024), SPP1+ TAMs, FOLR2+ TAMs (Rakina et al. 2024), and the resident vs. monocyte-derived distinction (Dalla et al. 2024). Mitsui and Satoh (2025) explicitly state the M1/M2 model is inadequate. This is not controversial.

**However**, there is a glaring internal contradiction that the team fails to address. Every single therapeutic recommendation in the Klein synthesis — CSF1R inhibitors, PI3K-gamma inhibitors, CD40 agonists, HDAC inhibitors, STING agonists — is framed in M1/M2 terms. The risk-benefit framework table uses columns labeled "M2-to-M1 repolarization." The dormancy risk assessment for each agent describes whether it shifts macrophages from "M2-like immunosuppressive" to "M1-like tumoricidal" states.

You cannot claim the M1/M2 binary is obsolete and then build your entire therapeutic strategy on it. If the spectrum model is correct — and I believe it is — then the team's therapeutic framework is incoherent, because none of these agents has been shown to produce a specific subset of macrophages that maintains dormancy while clearing proliferative DTCs. "Repolarization" toward an "M1-like state" is itself a binary concept applied to what the team acknowledges is a multidimensional continuum.

**Recommendation:** Either adopt the spectrum model consistently — which would require admitting that the therapeutic strategies are insufficiently characterized for dormancy-specific applications — or justify why the M1/M2 framework remains operationally useful despite being "obsolete." You cannot have it both ways.

### Claim 3: "TGF-beta is the single most important dormancy cytokine"

**Grade: MODERATE**

Moretti's report declares TGF-beta "the single most important dormancy cytokine" and claims it "appears across all briefings." The Klein report similarly lists TGF-beta2, BMP7, and WNT5A as dormancy-enforcing signals from M2-like macrophages.

This claim is problematic for several reasons:

1. **TGF-beta has dual roles that the team acknowledges but does not resolve.** Moretti's own Section IV lists "TGF-beta as both tumor suppressor and promoter" as a key contradiction. TGF-beta2 through TGF-betaRIII induces cytostatic dormancy (Dalla et al. 2024), but TGF-beta1 through NRP2 drives dormancy exit and proliferation (Recalde-Percaz et al. 2025). The team cannot simultaneously claim TGF-beta is the most important dormancy inducer and acknowledge that a closely related isoform drives dormancy exit, without specifying the conditions under which each effect dominates. "Context-dependent" is not an explanation; it is an admission of ignorance.

2. **IFN-gamma has equally strong mechanistic evidence.** Pereira et al. (2025) demonstrate that IFN-gamma directly induces dormancy via high p38/ERK ratio, activating STAT1. This was published in *Cell Reports*, a journal of equivalent stature to *Cell* (where Dalla et al. appeared). The team's own briefing (Vasquez) lists IFN-gamma as a "dominant dormancy inducer" alongside TGF-beta. Why is TGF-beta elevated above IFN-gamma?

3. **WNT5A, BMP4/7, and CXCL10 also have dormancy-enforcing evidence.** Zhang et al. (2025, *Cancer Metastasis Reviews*) lists WNT and HIPPO signaling alongside TGF-beta in dormancy regulation. The team cherry-picks TGF-beta for primacy without quantitative comparison.

4. **Most TGF-beta dormancy data are in vitro.** The in vivo evidence comes from Dalla et al. (2024), which examined TGF-beta2 specifically in the lung niche. Extrapolating lung-specific TGF-beta2 biology to "the single most important dormancy cytokine" across all organs is unwarranted.

**Recommendation:** Replace "the single most important dormancy cytokine" with "among the most consistently implicated dormancy-associated cytokines." Acknowledge that isoform-specific effects (TGF-beta1 vs. TGF-beta2), receptor-specific signaling (TGF-betaRIII vs. NRP2), and organ-specific contexts make this a complex landscape, not a single-cytokine story.

### Claim 4: "Chemotherapy (specifically taxanes) can paradoxically awaken dormant DTCs via IL-6/G-CSF-mediated MEK/ERK activation"

**Grade: MODERATE**

The mechanistic chain is coherent: taxane-induced stromal injury leads to IL-6/G-CSF release, which activates MEK/ERK signaling, shifting the ERK/p38 balance toward proliferation. Ganesan et al. (2023, *PLOS Biology*) provide the primary evidence.

However:

1. **This is a single study in a single chemotherapy class (taxanes).** The team generalizes to "chemotherapy" broadly. Do anthracyclines have the same effect? Platinum agents? Anti-metabolites? The team does not say, because the data do not exist. He et al. (2025, *Cancer Cell*) are cited by Okonkwo for platinum-induced NETs awakening dormant cells, but this is a different mechanism (NET-associated proteases degrading dormancy ECM, not IL-6/G-CSF-mediated ERK activation). These are two distinct pathways for two distinct drug classes. Treating them as one phenomenon is sloppy.

2. **The in vivo evidence is mouse-only.** Ganesan et al. used the D2.0R/D2A1 murine dormancy model and docetaxel treatment. This is a well-characterized model, but it is a model. Clinical evidence that adjuvant taxanes increase metastatic recurrence through dormancy exit is not presented. The team speculates but provides no data.

3. **The clinical implications are stated too strongly.** If adjuvant taxanes truly awakened dormant DTCs in patients, we would expect to see increased distant recurrence rates with taxane-containing regimens in clinical trials. We do not. ECOG 1199 and other large adjuvant taxane trials showed either benefit or neutral effect on distant recurrence-free survival. The team does not address this discrepancy.

**Recommendation:** Confine the chemotherapy paradox claim specifically to taxanes (and cite Ganesan et al. precisely). Acknowledge that clinical trial data do not currently support increased metastatic recurrence with taxane use. Discuss whether the mouse model may not capture the human clinical reality.

### Claim 5: "Alveolar macrophages are the gatekeepers of dormancy in the lung — tissue-resident, not monocyte-derived"

**Grade: STRONG (within its defined scope)**

Dalla et al. (2024, *Cell*) is the single strongest paper in this synthesis. The use of CD169-DTR mice to specifically deplete alveolar macrophages, combined with intravital imaging and clodronate controls, provides compelling evidence that tissue-resident alveolar macrophages maintain dormancy of breast cancer DTCs in the lung. The distinction between tissue-resident (dormancy-maintaining) and monocyte-derived (potentially outgrowth-promoting) macrophages is mechanistically important and supported by the data.

**Caveats:**

1. This is one organ, one cancer type, one mouse model. The team extrapolates freely to bone marrow, liver, and brain without comparable evidence.

2. The HER2/neu transgenic model is hormone-driven and immunogenic. Whether the same macrophage-dormancy axis operates in hormone receptor-negative or triple-negative breast cancer, or in non-breast cancers, is unknown.

3. The paper examines lung colonization after intravenous injection and spontaneous metastasis from the mammary fat pad. It does not address dormancy duration beyond the experimental timeline (weeks to months). Clinical dormancy can last decades.

**Recommendation:** Present Dalla et al. as a landmark study with strong mechanistic evidence for the lung niche specifically. Do not extrapolate. Explicitly state the limitations of the model system.

### Claim 6: "The ERK/p38 signaling ratio is the central intracellular dormancy switch"

**Grade: STRONG (for the concept) / MODERATE (for the oversimplification)**

The ERK/p38 ratio concept is well-established and originates from the foundational work of Aguirre-Ghiso and colleagues over two decades. It is reproduced across multiple systems. All five postdocs and both senior scientists converge on it. This convergence is genuine.

However:

1. **The ERK/p38 ratio is necessary but not sufficient to explain dormancy.** NR2F1 acts upstream. WNT signaling acts in parallel. HIPPO/YAP signaling is implicated (Dai et al. 2022, brain niche). Autophagy pathways contribute. The team presents ERK/p38 as *the* switch when it is *a* switch embedded in a larger network.

2. **The ratio is measured differently across studies.** Phospho-specific Western blots, immunofluorescence, and flow cytometry all produce different quantitative values. There is no standardized threshold for "dormant" vs. "proliferative" ERK/p38 ratios across experimental systems. The team treats the ratio as a binary switch when the underlying biology is continuous.

3. **Downstream effectors are underspecified.** What happens after ERK/p38 shifts? The team lists STAT1, cyclin D1, c-Myc, Bcl-2 as downstream targets, but the causal chain from ERK/p38 to actual cell cycle arrest or re-entry is not fully mapped in the dormancy context. p27 and DEC2 are mentioned as dormancy effectors, but their relationship to the ERK/p38 ratio is presented as established fact without citing the specific evidence connecting them.

**Recommendation:** Retain the ERK/p38 framework but present it as the best-characterized component of a multi-pathway dormancy network. Acknowledge that the ratio itself is an oversimplification of what is almost certainly a multi-dimensional signaling space.

### Claim 7: "CSF1R inhibitors carry the highest risk of paradoxical dormancy exit; PI3K-gamma inhibitors (eganelisib) have the most favorable risk-benefit profile"

**Grade: WEAK**

This is the therapeutic claim most in need of dismantling.

1. **There are zero dormancy-specific preclinical data for any macrophage-targeting agent.** The Klein report admits this explicitly: "No macrophage-targeting therapy has been evaluated in a clinical trial with dormancy-specific endpoints." The risk-benefit framework table is therefore a ranking of theoretical risks based on mechanism-of-action reasoning, not empirical evidence. It is an informed opinion, not a data-driven assessment. Presenting it as a ranked framework gives it a false patina of quantification.

2. **The distinction between "depletion" (CSF1R) and "repolarization" (PI3K-gamma) is presented as clean and clear.** It is neither. PI3K-gamma inhibition does not exclusively repolarize macrophages; it also affects neutrophils, myeloid-derived suppressor cells, and T cells. O'Connell et al. (2024) documented immune activation beyond macrophage repolarization in the MARIO-3 trial. The assumption that PI3K-gamma inhibition selectively alters dormancy-relevant macrophage signals without affecting other niche components is untested.

3. **Eganelisib's clinical development is problematic.** The MARIO-1 trial (Hong et al. 2023) was a Phase 1/1b study. The MARIO-3 trial was a Phase 2 study in frontline metastatic TNBC. Both tested the drug in advanced, bulky disease — the opposite of the dormancy/MRD context. Infinity Pharmaceuticals, the drug's developer, has an unclear operational status. The team is proposing a Phase 2 adjuvant trial of a drug whose developer may not exist to sponsor it.

4. **The Watson et al. (2024, *Cancer Cell*) fibrotic compensation finding is cited for CSF1R inhibitors but not tested for PI3K-gamma inhibitors.** If macrophage repolarization triggers compensatory fibrosis (as macrophage depletion does), eganelisib's risk profile may be higher than the team claims. The team does not know because the experiment has not been done.

**Recommendation:** Downgrade eganelisib from "lead therapeutic candidate" to "candidate warranting dormancy-specific preclinical testing." Remove the Phase 2 clinical trial proposal until Phase 0/1 dormancy data exist. Explicitly state that the risk-benefit framework is theoretical, not empirical.

### Claim 8: "The CD68/CD163 ratio is a validated clinical biomarker for dormancy risk (HR 10.32)"

**Grade: WEAK**

This is one of the most damaging weaknesses in the entire synthesis, and the team does not appear to recognize it.

Darvishian et al. (2022, *The Breast*): the study had **67 patients total, of whom only 13 experienced recurrence.** The HR of 10.32 for CD68/CD163 ratio >0.46 predicting DCIS recurrence comes from a Cox proportional hazards model with 13 events. The 95% confidence interval is **1.34 to 80.0**. Let me state what this means: the true hazard ratio could be as low as 1.34 (clinically insignificant) or as high as 80 (enormous). The point estimate of 10.32 is essentially meaningless with a confidence interval spanning two orders of magnitude. This is a preliminary finding in a tiny cohort, not a validated biomarker.

Furthermore:

1. The study is in DCIS (ductal carcinoma in situ), not invasive cancer with dormant DTCs. The team extrapolates from DCIS recurrence to dormant DTC reactivation without justification.

2. No independent validation cohort exists. A biomarker is not "validated" until it has been confirmed in at least one independent dataset. The team cites Parmar et al. (2026) for TMEM scores but provides no independent validation of the CD68/CD163 ratio.

3. CD68 and CD163 are pan-macrophage and M2-macrophage markers, respectively, but neither is specific to the macrophage subsets that enforce dormancy. The team's own spectrum model argues against using binary markers for complex phenotypes.

**Recommendation:** Remove the word "validated." Present the CD68/CD163 ratio as a "preliminary finding requiring independent validation in larger cohorts." State the actual confidence interval. Stop citing HR 10.32 without its confidence interval — this is misleading.

### Claim 9: "M2 macrophage exosomes maintain dormancy via gap junctional intercellular communication (GJIC); M1 exosomes break dormancy via NF-kappaB"

**Grade: MODERATE (with serious caveats)**

This claim derives almost entirely from Walker et al. (2019, *Cell Death & Disease*). It is a single study. The team treats it as established fact across multiple reports.

1. **Walker et al. used in vitro co-culture systems with breast cancer cell lines.** The GJIC mechanism was demonstrated in culture dishes. Whether GJIC operates between macrophage exosomes and dormant DTCs in vivo — in the lung, bone marrow, liver, or brain — has not been demonstrated.

2. **Gap junctional communication requires specific connexin expression on both donor and recipient cells.** The team does not specify which connexins are involved, whether dormant DTCs express the complementary connexins, or whether the spatial constraints of the in vivo niche permit exosome-mediated GJIC. This is a mechanistic claim with substantial unresolved molecular prerequisites.

3. **The M1/M2 exosome distinction is itself binary.** See my critique of Claim 2. If macrophages exist on a spectrum, exosome cargo should reflect that spectrum. The clean binary of "M2 exosomes maintain dormancy, M1 exosomes break it" is almost certainly an oversimplification of what in vivo macrophage-derived exosomes actually carry.

4. **No other group has independently replicated the GJIC-mediated dormancy maintenance finding.** This is a 2019 paper with 154 citations (according to Bergstrom's report). Citation count alone does not establish validity, but the absence of independent replication of the specific GJIC mechanism is notable.

**Recommendation:** Present the Walker et al. GJIC finding as a provocative but unreplicated in vitro observation. Do not present it as an established in vivo mechanism. Flag it as a priority for independent validation.

### Claim 10: "Selective macrophage repolarization is strongly preferred over broad depletion for the dormancy context"

**Grade: UNSUPPORTED (as stated)**

The word "selective" is doing enormous unjustified work here. Selective repolarization requires that one can:

(a) identify the specific macrophage subsets that maintain dormancy,
(b) distinguish them from subsets that promote outgrowth,
(c) pharmacologically target one subset without affecting the other, and
(d) do so across multiple organ niches with distinct macrophage ontogenies.

None of these conditions have been met. The team admits in multiple places that "which specific subsets enforce or break dormancy is unknown" (Klein report, Section VIII.B.2), that "no study has profiled macrophages in direct contact with dormant vs. proliferative DTCs" (Moretti report, Section VII.3), and that "single-cell RNA sequencing has identified multiple TAM subsets... but which specific subsets enforce or break dormancy is unknown" (Klein report, Section VIII.B.2).

If you do not know which macrophage subsets to target, you cannot claim that "selective repolarization" is possible, let alone "strongly preferred." What the team actually means is: "Repolarization is theoretically preferable to depletion, but we cannot achieve selectivity because we do not know what to select for." This is an honest admission buried under a dishonest headline.

**Recommendation:** Replace "strongly preferred" with "theoretically preferable but currently unachievable due to the inability to identify dormancy-enforcing vs. dormancy-breaking macrophage subsets." Acknowledge that until dormancy-specific macrophage subsets are identified, all therapeutic strategies — whether depletion or repolarization — carry unpredictable dormancy-disruption risk.

## SECTION 2: IDENTIFIED MISCONCEPTIONS

### Misconception 1: Correlation Between Macrophage Presence and Dormancy Equals Causation

Throughout the synthesis, the co-localization of macrophages with dormant DTCs is presented as evidence that macrophages regulate dormancy. Dalla et al. (2024) provide the strongest causal evidence (macrophage depletion causes dormancy exit), but the other niche stories rely on correlative data. The bone marrow niche (osteomacs near dormant DTCs), liver niche (Kupffer cells interacting with HSCs), and brain niche (microglia in the vicinity of dormant cells) are all described as if spatial association implies regulatory function. This is the oldest error in cell biology.

### Misconception 2: In Vitro Macrophage-DTC Co-culture Results Transfer to In Vivo Niches

Walker et al. (2019) GJIC findings, Zheng et al. (2023) HDAC2-SP1 findings, and most cytokine signaling data come from in vitro systems. The in vivo niche is a three-dimensional, multi-cellular, vascularized, mechanically stressed, immunologically active environment. The concentration gradients, oxygen tensions, metabolic substrate availability, and cell-cell contact geometries in a culture dish bear no resemblance to a lung alveolus or bone marrow sinusoid. The team makes no systematic effort to flag which findings are in vitro-only and which have in vivo confirmation.

### Misconception 3: Mouse Dormancy Models Accurately Reflect Human Clinical Dormancy

The D2.0R/D2A1 system, HER2/neu transgenic mice, and PyMT models are all standard tools. They model dormancy over weeks to months. Human clinical dormancy lasts years to decades. The molecular mechanisms that maintain quiescence for 3 weeks in a mouse may not be the same mechanisms that maintain quiescence for 15 years in a patient. The team does not address the temporal scaling problem.

### Misconception 4: "Repolarization" Is a Well-Defined Pharmacological Concept

The team uses "repolarization" as if it describes a specific, measurable pharmacological transition. In reality, "repolarization" is a catch-all term for any phenotypic shift in macrophages, measured by any combination of surface markers, cytokine profiles, or functional assays. There is no consensus on what constitutes successful "repolarization" in the dormancy context, because the relevant functional readout (maintenance of DTC quiescence) has not been measured for any repolarization agent.

### Misconception 5: The Post-Surgical Vulnerability Window Is a Clinically Validated Concept

Chen's report describes a "post-surgical vulnerability window" of "1 week to 6 months" during which dormant DTCs may be reactivated. The primary citation is Tang et al. (2020, *Clinical and Translational Medicine*). Klein's own citation verification flags this reference as "INCONCLUSIVE" — it could not be independently confirmed. More critically, even if the citation is valid, Tang et al. 2020 is a **review article**, not a primary research study. The "vulnerability window" concept is an attractive hypothesis supported by biological plausibility, but it has not been validated in a prospective clinical study with dormancy-specific endpoints. Presenting it as a defined temporal window with specific day-by-day phases (Days 0-7: "peak immunosuppression," Days 7-14: "immune restoration begins") is an exercise in false precision.

### Misconception 6: Single-Cell RNA-seq Signatures Define Functional Macrophage States

The team cites TREM2+, SPP1+, FOLR2+, and MARCO+ TAM subsets as if scRNA-seq clusters correspond to functional dormancy-relevant states. Transcriptomic clustering identifies cells with similar gene expression profiles; it does not establish that these profiles correspond to distinct functional behaviors toward DTCs. A TREM2+ macrophage may or may not maintain dormancy. An SPP1+ macrophage may or may not promote outgrowth. The mapping from transcriptomic signature to dormancy function has not been performed. The team conflates cataloguing with understanding.

## SECTION 3: METHODOLOGICAL CRITIQUE

### 3.1 The D2.0R/D2A1 Murine Dormancy Model Is Insufficiently Validated

The D2.0R (dormancy-prone) and D2A1 (proliferative) murine mammary tumor cell lines are the workhorses of the dormancy field. They are used by Ganesan et al. (2023), Walker et al. (2019), and presumably Dalla et al. (2024). However:

- These are mouse-derived cell lines with defined oncogenic lesions. They do not represent the heterogeneity of human breast cancer subtypes.
- D2.0R cells are selected for their dormancy phenotype. This is a selection artifact that may not reflect the dormancy programs of spontaneously arising DTCs.
- The dormancy observed in these models (weeks to months) does not recapitulate clinical dormancy duration (years to decades).

The team relies on this model system for multiple mechanistic and therapeutic claims without acknowledging its limitations.

### 3.2 Clodronate Liposomes as Macrophage Depletion Tools Are Non-Specific

Dalla et al. (2024) used both clodronate liposomes and CD169-DTR genetic depletion to deplete alveolar macrophages. Clodronate liposomes deplete all phagocytic cells, not just macrophages. Dendritic cells, monocytes, and other phagocytes are also affected. The CD169-DTR system is more specific (CD169 is expressed on alveolar macrophages), but CD169 is also expressed on marginal zone macrophages in the spleen and some dendritic cell subsets. The possibility that observed effects are due to depletion of non-macrophage phagocytes is not adequately discussed.

### 3.3 The ERK/p38 Ratio Is Measured by Different Methods Across Studies

Western blot quantification of phospho-ERK and phospho-p38 is semi-quantitative at best. Different studies use different antibodies, different cell lysis conditions, different time points, and different normalization strategies. Treating the ERK/p38 ratio as a precise numerical switch across studies that used incompatible measurement approaches is methodologically unsound.

### 3.4 Exosome Characterization Is Inconsistent

Walker et al. (2019) characterized exosomes from in vitro-polarized macrophages. The exosome field has been plagued by reproducibility concerns, contamination with non-exosomal extracellular vesicles, and inconsistent characterization standards. The International Society for Extracellular Vesicles (ISEV) has published guidelines (MISEV2018, updated MISEV2023) that set minimum characterization standards. It is unclear whether Walker et al. meet these standards, and the team does not discuss exosome characterization rigor at all.

### 3.5 No Negative Control Experiments Are Discussed

For a synthesis that makes causal claims about macrophage regulation of dormancy, the absence of discussion about negative controls is striking. For example:

- What happens when macrophages are present but their secreted factors are neutralized? This would distinguish contact-dependent from soluble factor-mediated effects.
- What happens when other immune cells are depleted in the same models? If NK cell depletion also causes dormancy exit, the macrophage-specificity claim weakens.
- What happens when macrophages are added back to a depleted niche? Rescue experiments would strengthen the causality argument.

The team does not discuss any of these controls, either because the original studies did not perform them or because the team did not think to ask.

### 3.6 Spatial Transcriptomics Is Invoked but Never Applied

Both senior scientist reports recommend "spatial transcriptomics collaboration for macrophage-DTC pairs" (Moretti) and "spatial multi-omics of the dormant niche" (Klein/Chen/Patel). These are excellent recommendations. They also highlight the absence of any existing spatial transcriptomics data in the synthesis. Every claim about macrophage-DTC interactions in specific niches is based on either in vitro co-culture, bulk tissue analysis, or imaging of cell-type markers — none of which provides the spatial resolution needed to confirm that specific macrophage subsets are in direct contact with dormant DTCs and signaling to them.

## SECTION 4: ALTERNATIVE INTERPRETATIONS

### 4.1 Macrophages May Be Passive Bystanders, Not Active Regulators

An alternative interpretation of Dalla et al. (2024) is that alveolar macrophage depletion disrupts the lung microenvironment (cytokine milieu, ECM integrity, immune surveillance) in ways that are not specific to macrophage-DTC signaling. The dormant DTCs may re-enter the cell cycle because the niche is destabilized, not because a specific macrophage-derived signal was withdrawn. The team does not consider this alternative.

### 4.2 TGF-beta May Be a Symptom, Not a Cause, of Dormancy

If dormant DTCs create a quiescent, hypoxic niche, the resulting microenvironment may favor TGF-beta production by multiple cell types (fibroblasts, endothelial cells, epithelial cells) — not just macrophages. Elevated TGF-beta in dormancy niches could be a consequence of the quiescent state, not its cause. The team assumes directionality without establishing it.

### 4.3 The Angiogenic Switch May Be Independent of Macrophage Polarization

The team attributes the angiogenic switch (VEGF, bFGF, MMP-9 from M2-like macrophages; TSP-1 from M1-like macrophages) to macrophage polarization state. However, angiogenic dormancy may be primarily regulated by endothelial cell-DTC interactions, with macrophage-derived angiogenic factors being contributory but not decisive. The team does not consider the possibility that macrophage-independent angiogenic regulation dominates.

### 4.4 Chemotherapy-Induced Awakening May Reflect Direct Stromal Toxicity, Not Macrophage Repolarization

Ganesan et al. (2023) show that docetaxel injures bone marrow stromal cells, releasing IL-6/G-CSF. The team interprets this through the macrophage lens (macrophage repolarization, M2 expansion). An equally valid interpretation is that direct stromal injury — independent of macrophages — releases proliferative signals. The increase in M2 macrophages may be a response to tissue damage, not a cause of dormancy exit. The team's macrophage-centric framework biases interpretation toward macrophage-mediated mechanisms.

### 4.5 The CSF-1/EGF Paracrine Loop May Be a Cancer Cell Autonomous Phenomenon

The EGF/CSF-1 paracrine loop (DTCs secrete CSF-1, macrophages reciprocate with EGF) is presented as a bidirectional interaction. However, many cancer cells express both CSF-1 and EGFR and can autocrinely signal. The extent to which dormancy regulation depends on the paracrine loop (requiring macrophages) vs. autocrine signaling (cancer cell autonomous) is not resolved.

### 4.6 Stromal Compensation May Nullify Any Macrophage-Targeting Strategy

Watson et al. (2024) demonstrated fibrotic compensation after CSF1R inhibition in glioblastoma. This suggests that the tissue niche has homeostatic mechanisms that resist perturbation. If macrophage depletion or repolarization triggers compensatory fibrosis, inflammation, or recruitment of other immunosuppressive cells (MDSCs, Tregs), the net effect on dormant DTCs may be neutral or harmful regardless of the strategy chosen. The team does not grapple with the possibility that the stroma will simply compensate for any macrophage-directed intervention.

## SECTION 5: MISSING EVIDENCE

### 5.1 No Dormancy-Specific Macrophage Subset Has Been Identified

This is the single most important gap, and the team acknowledges it in passing without treating it as the fatal weakness it represents. Without knowing which macrophage subset(s) enforce dormancy, all therapeutic strategies are flying blind. The scRNA-seq studies cited (Coulton et al. 2024, Rakina et al. 2024, Klughammer et al. 2024) profile TAMs in established tumors, not in dormant niches. No study has performed single-cell analysis of macrophages in direct physical contact with dormant vs. proliferative DTCs.

### 5.2 No Time-Course Data on Macrophage Polarization During DTC Awakening

The temporal dynamics of macrophage phenotype switching during dormancy exit are completely unknown. Does macrophage repolarization precede, coincide with, or follow DTC awakening? The team recommends "temporal dynamics of niche repolarization" as a future study but does not treat this as the critical prerequisite it is. If macrophage polarization changes after DTCs have already begun proliferating, then targeting macrophages will not prevent awakening — it will be too late.

### 5.3 No Human Dormancy Data for Any Claim

Every mechanistic claim in this synthesis is based on mouse models or in vitro systems. No human data demonstrate that macrophage polarization state in any organ correlates with dormancy duration, awakening risk, or metastatic recurrence timing. The closest the team comes is the Darvishian et al. (2022) CD68/CD163 ratio study, which is in DCIS (not dormant DTCs) with 67 patients and 13 events. This is not human dormancy data.

### 5.4 No Pharmacokinetic/Pharmacodynamic Data for Macrophage-Targeting Agents in Dormancy Niches

The team proposes eganelisib as the lead candidate. There are no data on whether eganelisib reaches the bone marrow, lung alveoli, liver sinusoids, or brain parenchyma at concentrations sufficient to repolarize tissue-resident macrophages. The MARIO-1 and MARIO-3 trials measured drug effects in breast tumor tissue. Dormant niches are in different organs with different drug penetration profiles.

### 5.5 No Evidence That "Maintain Dormancy" Is Superior to "Awaken and Eliminate"

The team assumes that maintaining dormancy is the preferred therapeutic strategy. This assumption is untested. An alternative school of thought argues that forced awakening combined with immune-mediated clearance may be more effective, because lifelong dormancy maintenance therapy is impractical. The team dismisses the "awaken and eliminate" approach as "high-risk" without quantitative evidence comparing the two strategies.

### 5.6 No Cross-Organ Comparison of Macrophage-Dormancy Mechanisms

The team describes lung, bone marrow, liver, and brain niches as if they operate by similar macrophage-dependent mechanisms. No study has systematically compared macrophage function across these niches in the same experimental system. The lung story (Dalla et al. 2024) may not apply to bone marrow, liver, or brain. The team does not know, because the experiments have not been done.

### 5.7 No Mathematical or Computational Model of the Dormancy-Macrophage Interaction

Given the complexity of the signaling networks described (ERK/p38, TGF-beta isoforms, STAT1/STAT3, NF-kappaB, WNT, HIPPO, Notch, exosomal miRNAs, GJIC, ECM remodeling), a systems biology or computational model could help predict the outcome of therapeutic interventions. No such model is discussed or proposed. The team is building a qualitative framework when quantitative prediction is needed.

The CD68/CD163 ratio, TMEM doorway scores, and any other proposed clinical biomarkers should undergo formal statistical review before being presented as "clinical correlates" with specific hazard ratios. The current presentation is statistically naive.

### Directive 10: Acknowledge the Temporal Scaling Problem

Add a section to each senior scientist report addressing the mismatch between mouse dormancy timescales (weeks to months) and human clinical dormancy timescales (years to decades). Discuss whether mechanisms identified in short-term mouse models are likely to be relevant to long-term human dormancy.

## SECTION 7: DEVIL'S ADVOCATE SUMMARY — THE FIVE MOST DAMAGING CRITICISMS

### Damage Point 1: The Citation Integrity Problem Undermines the Entire Evidence Base

Three citations are unverifiable or hallucinated (He et al. 2026, Sun et al. 2023, Tang et al. 2020 flagged as inconclusive). One citation is attributed to the wrong journal (Zheng et al. 2023 in *Cancer Discovery* instead of *Cancer Research*). In a formal peer review, this would trigger an immediate editorial investigation for citation fraud. Even assuming good faith (sloppy record-keeping rather than fabrication), the presence of four problematic citations across five postdocs and two senior scientists suggests a systemic quality control failure in the literature verification process. If the team cannot correctly cite their sources, why should anyone trust their interpretation of those sources?

### Damage Point 2: The CD68/CD163 Biomarker Claim Is Statistically Invalid

The HR 10.32 headline figure, cited prominently across multiple reports as evidence that macrophage polarization is clinically relevant to recurrence, comes from a study with 67 patients and 13 events. The 95% confidence interval spans from 1.34 to 80.0. This is not a validated biomarker; it is a preliminary finding that could be entirely due to chance. Building a clinical development roadmap that includes CD68/CD163 ratio as a patient stratification tool for an adjuvant trial, based on this single underpowered study, is scientifically irresponsible.

### Damage Point 3: The Lead Therapeutic Candidate Has Zero Dormancy-Specific Data and an Uncertain Commercial Future

Eganelisib is proposed as the "lead therapeutic candidate" based on the MARIO-1 and MARIO-3 clinical trials. These trials tested the drug in advanced metastatic disease, not in the adjuvant/MRD/dormancy setting. The drug has never been tested in any dormancy model. Its developer, Infinity Pharmaceuticals, has an unclear operational status. The team is proposing to build a Phase 2 adjuvant trial around a drug that (a) has no dormancy-relevant preclinical data, (b) has no dormancy-relevant clinical data, and (c) may have no company to manufacture or sponsor it. This is not a clinical development plan; it is a fantasy.

### Damage Point 4: The Team Cannot Simultaneously Reject and Rely on the M1/M2 Binary

The synthesis declares the M1/M2 framework "insufficient" and "obsolete" in one section and then uses it as the organizing principle for the entire therapeutic strategy in another. Every risk-benefit assessment, every therapeutic recommendation, and every proposed clinical trial design is framed in M1/M2 terms. This internal contradiction is not a minor inconsistency; it reveals that the team's conceptual framework is incoherent. If the spectrum model is correct, the therapeutic framework needs to be rebuilt from scratch using subset-specific language and subset-specific data — which do not currently exist.

### Damage Point 5: There Are No Human Data Supporting Any Mechanistic Claim

Every mechanistic claim in this synthesis — macrophage polarization governs dormancy, TGF-beta is the key cytokine, the ERK/p38 ratio is the intracellular switch, alveolar macrophages are dormancy gatekeepers, CSF-1 signaling drives dissemination — is based on mouse models and in vitro systems. Zero human studies have validated any of these mechanisms in the context of dormant DTCs. Zero. The closest the team comes is a 67-patient DCIS study with 13 events and a statistically meaningless hazard ratio. The entire synthesis is a tower of preclinical observations presented with the confidence of clinical evidence. It is not clinical evidence. It is a preclinical literature review with delusions of translational grandeur.

## FINAL ASSESSMENT

This research synthesis identifies several genuinely important papers and asks the right high-level questions. The literature coverage is broad and mostly accurate (citation errors notwithstanding). The recognition that the M1/M2 binary is inadequate is correct. The identification of Dalla et al. (2024) as a cornerstone study is sound.

But the synthesis collapses under the weight of its own ambition. It extrapolates from one organ to all organs, from one cancer type to all cancers, from weeks in mice to decades in humans, from in vitro co-culture to in vivo physiology, from mechanism-of-action reasoning to ranked therapeutic frameworks, and from a 67-patient study with 13 events to clinical trial design. Each extrapolation is presented with confidence rather than caution.

The team needs to do three things before this work is ready for any external audience:

1. **Fix the citations.** Every reference must be verified at the PMID level. Incorrect journal attributions must be corrected. Hallucinated citations must be removed and replaced.

2. **Separate what is known from what is speculated.** Use consistent, explicit language. "Established in mouse models" is not the same as "established." "Consistent with" is not the same as "demonstrates." "Theoretically preferable" is not the same as "strongly preferred."

3. **Abandon the premature clinical development roadmap.** Replace it with a prioritized list of preclinical experiments. The first item on that list should be: identify the macrophage subset(s) that enforce dormancy in each organ niche, using spatial transcriptomics of macrophage-DTC pairs from dormant vs. reactivated niches. Until that experiment is done, all therapeutic speculation is premature.

I am available for follow-up review after revisions.
